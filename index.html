HS teacher turned UX designer

I’ve spent the last decade as a teacher, working in underserved classrooms in New York, Chicago and the Bay Area. My time in schools has been spent trying to narrow the education gap through daily lessons, student one-on-ones and parent teacher conferences. I cherish the relationships I’ve built with students, relishing in their success, supporting them in sports and chaperoning their dances, which were mostly great….sometimes gross.

Most recently, I worked as a College Counselor supporting high school seniors through the college application and financial aid processes. I’ve always been drawn to education because of my belief in its power. I’ve also realized, however, that I no longer belong inside the four walls of a classroom.

This reality presented itself after the graduation of what may be my favorite class ever - the class or 2015 - a truly remarkable group of kids. During this year, I was forced to get creative with technology to meet the needs of all seniors:

I used Google apps to create tools that helped students interpret what their GPA and test scores mean for their college options;

I screencasted directional videos explaining various pieces of the college application process so students could work at their own pace;

I built tracking tools to help them manage college applications and scholarships.

I also created tools for teachers and students at every grade level to converse about college, helping them understand each student’s college eligibility and how to improve it.

Though I adored my students in the class or 2015, I recognized that the time I loved most was designing tools that enriched their experience and empowered them - so often I lost track of time. It felt like my most impactful year and I wondered…

...could this be my job?

Through research I discovered user experience design - a field dedicated to thoughtful building to solve the needs of users. I have a continued commitment to education, yet I’ve been able to explore other interests through my design and gain new curiosities.

Turns out, it is a job, and I now spend my days losing track of time.

And although I miss student interactions, I find comfort in daily interaction with my own child, Sullivan, though not yet a student, he most certainly loves to dance and is gross everyday.
